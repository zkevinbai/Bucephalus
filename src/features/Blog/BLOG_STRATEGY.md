# Blog Content Strategy

## Voice & Tone

### Core Principles

**Direct and Clear**
- No jargon. No hedging. Say what you mean.
- Use simple language, even for complex topics.
- "Technology is the means, not the goal." Not "Technology should be considered as a means rather than an end goal."

**Engineer's Perspective**
- Write from experience, not theory.
- Ground arguments in real examples: "I've watched projects fail because engineers couldn't stop perfecting."
- Show, don't tell. Use concrete examples over abstract concepts.

**Practical Over Perfect**
- Focus on what works, not what's theoretically optimal.
- Acknowledge messy reality: "Every company runs their business differently."
- Value execution over perfection: "Ship something that works. Iterate from there."

**Confident but Not Arrogant**
- Make strong statements: "This counterintuitive truth has played out across industries for centuries."
- Support with evidence, not qualifiers.
- Trust the reader to understand nuance without hedging.

### Writing Style

**Opening Hooks**
- Start with a strong, declarative statement:
  - "When costs drop, people don't buy less‚Äîthey spend the same and buy more."
  - "Technology is the means, not the goal."
  - "MCPs are quietly replacing Postman and curl‚Äîand most people haven't noticed yet."
- Get to the point immediately. No long wind-ups.

**Paragraph Structure**
- Short paragraphs (2-4 sentences typically).
- One idea per paragraph.
- Use spacing to create rhythm and emphasis.

**Section Headings**
- Clear, descriptive headings that preview the content.
- Use "The [Concept]" format for major sections: "The Jevons Paradox", "The Cost Paradox", "The Real-World Moat"
- Use numbered lists or emoji for sub-sections when helpful: "üéØ 1. Consultant: Turning Understanding Into Trust"

**Examples and Evidence**
- Use real companies: Microsoft, Oracle, SAP, Stripe, Epic, Salesforce, Palantir
- Use historical analogies: 1998 software companies, open-source revolution, on-prem to SaaS transition
- Use concrete scenarios: "When someone asks about payroll calculations..."
- Mix specific and general examples appropriately

**Language Patterns**
- Prefer active voice: "Software companies can access foundation models" not "Foundation models can be accessed by software companies"
- Use contractions naturally: "don't", "can't", "won't"
- Avoid corporate speak: "leverage", "synergy", "paradigm shift"
- Use strong verbs: "build", "solve", "win" not "facilitate", "enable", "optimize"

**Building Technology**
- What makes technology effective

### Content Approach

**Use Historical Analogies**
- Draw parallels to past transitions (on-prem to SaaS, open-source revolution)
- Show patterns that repeat: "The same pattern appears everywhere."
- Learn from what worked: "Microsoft, Oracle, SAP, Adobe, Intuit‚Äîthey all successfully transitioned."

**Focus on Real-World Complexity**
- Acknowledge that software exists in messy reality
- Regulatory requirements, money movement, insurance, tax systems
- These aren't software problems‚Äîthey're real-world problems that software must solve

**Explain the "Why"**
- Don't just state what happens‚Äîexplain why it matters
- Connect technical concepts to business outcomes
- Show the implications: "This creates a moat that foundation model companies cannot match."

## Structure Guidelines

### Post Length
- Target 5-8 minute reads
- Longer posts (8+ min) are fine if the content warrants it
- Shorter posts (3-5 min) work for focused topics

### Post Organization

**Opening**
- Strong hook (1-2 paragraphs)
- Set up the core argument or observation

**Body**
- 3-5 major sections with clear headings
- Each section builds on the previous
- Use examples to illustrate points
- Mix specific examples with general principles

**Conclusion**
- Reinforce the main point
- End with a forward-looking statement or call to action
- Keep it brief‚Äîdon't rehash everything

### Example Structure (from existing posts)

**"The Cost Paradox"**
1. Opening hook: The counterintuitive truth
2. Historical proof: Jevons Paradox
3. Software examples: Open-source revolution, on-prem to SaaS
4. Application: What this means for AI era
5. Conclusion: The pattern holds

**"Where Foundation Models Can't Beat Software Companies"**
1. Opening: The asymmetric advantage
2. Why software companies win: Access to models
3. Deep access requirement: Why it matters
4. Examples: Rippling, Salesforce, Palantir
5. The moat: Controlling access
6. Conclusion: The advantage

## Final Notes

**Write for yourself first**
- If you find it interesting, others will too
- Don't write what you think people want to hear
- Write what you've learned and what you believe

**Trust your experience**
- You've worked across five continents
- You've seen what works and what doesn't
- Your perspective is valuable‚Äîshare it directly

**Keep it simple**
- Complex ideas can be explained simply
